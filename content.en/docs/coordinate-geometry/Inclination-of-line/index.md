## 5.4 Inclination of a Line

The **inclination** of a line or the **angle of inclination** of a line is the angle which a straight line makes with the positive direction of X-axis measured in the counter-clockwise direction to the part of the line above the X-axis. The inclination of the line is usually denoted by {{< katex display=true >}}
\theta
{{< /katex >}}.

> **Note:**
> - The inclination of X-axis and every line parallel to X-axis is {{< katex display=true >}}
0°
{{< /katex >}}.
> - The inclination of Y-axis and every line parallel to Y-axis is {{< katex display=true >}}
90°
{{< /katex >}}.

### 5.4.1 Slope of a Straight line

While laying roads one must know how steep the road will be. Similarly, when constructing a staircase, we should consider its steepness. For the same reason, anyone travelling along a hill or a bridge, feels hard compared to travelling along a plain road.

All these examples illustrate one important aspect called **"Steepness"**. The measure of steepness is called **slope** or **gradient**.

The concept of slope is important in economics because it is used to measure the rate at which the demand for a product changes in a given period of time on the basis of its price. Slope comprises of two factors namely steepness and direction.

#### Definition

If {{< katex display=true >}}
\theta{{< katex display=true >}}
 is the angle of inclination of a non-vertical straight line, then {{< katex display=true >}}
\tan\theta
{{< /katex >}} is called the **slope** or **gradient** of the line and is denoted by 
{{< /katex >}}m
{{< /katex >}}.

Therefore the slope of the straight line is:

{{< katex display=true >}}
m = \tan\theta, \quad 0° \leq \theta \leq 180°, \quad \theta \neq 90°
{{< /katex >}}

#### To find the slope of a straight line when two points are given

{{< katex display=true >}}
\text{Slope } m = \tan\theta = \frac{\text{opposite side}}{\text{adjacent side}} = \frac{BC}{AC} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\text{Slope } m = \frac{\text{Difference in y coordinates}}{\text{Difference in x coordinates}}
{{< /katex >}}

> **Note:**
> - The slope of a vertical line is **undefined**.
> - The slope of the line through {{< katex display=true >}}
(x_1, y_1){{< katex display=true >}}
 and {{< katex display=true >}}
(x_2, y_2){{< katex display=true >}}
 with 
{{< /katex >}}x_1 \neq x_2
{{< /katex >}} is 
{{< /katex >}}\frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}
{{< /katex >}}.

## 5.4.2 Slopes of parallel lines

Two non-vertical lines are parallel if and only if their slopes are equal.

Let {{< katex display=true >}}
l_1{{< katex display=true >}}
 and {{< katex display=true >}}
l_2{{< katex display=true >}}
 be two non-vertical lines with slopes 
{{< /katex >}}m_1
{{< /katex >}} and 
{{< /katex >}}m_2
{{< /katex >}} respectively.

Let the inclination of the lines with positive direction of X axis be {{< katex display=true >}}
\theta_1{{< katex display=true >}}
 and 
{{< /katex >}}\theta_2
{{< /katex >}} respectively.

Assume, {{< katex display=true >}}
l_1{{< katex display=true >}}
 and 
{{< /katex >}}l_2
{{< /katex >}} are parallel.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\theta_1 = \theta_2 \text{ (Since, } \theta_1, \theta_2 \text{ are corresponding angles)}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\tan\theta_1 = \tan\theta_2
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
m_1 = m_2
{{< /katex >}}

Hence, the slopes are equal. Therefore, non-vertical parallel lines have equal slopes.

### Conversely

Let the slopes be equal, then {{< katex display=true >}}
m_1 = m_2
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\tan\theta_1 = \tan\theta_2
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\theta_1 = \theta_2 \text{ (since } 0° \leq \theta_1, \theta_2 \leq 180°)
{{< /katex >}}

That is the corresponding angles are equal.

Therefore, {{< katex display=true >}}
l_1{{< katex display=true >}}
 and 
{{< /katex >}}l_2
{{< /katex >}} are parallel.

Thus, non-vertical lines having equal slopes are parallel.

**Hence, non vertical lines are parallel if and only if their slopes are equal.**

## 5.4.3 Slopes of perpendicular lines

Two non-vertical lines with slopes {{< katex display=true >}}
m_1{{< katex display=true >}}
 and {{< katex display=true >}}
m_2
{{< /katex >}} are perpendicular if and only if 
{{< /katex >}}m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1
{{< /katex >}}.

Let {{< katex display=true >}}
l_1{{< katex display=true >}}
 and {{< katex display=true >}}
l_2{{< katex display=true >}}
 be two non-vertical lines with slopes {{< katex display=true >}}
m_1{{< katex display=true >}}
 and 
{{< /katex >}}m_2
{{< /katex >}} respectively. Let their inclinations be 
{{< /katex >}}\theta_1
{{< /katex >}} and 
{{< /katex >}}\theta_2
{{< /katex >}} respectively.

Then {{< katex display=true >}}
m_1 = \tan\theta_1{{< katex display=true >}}
 and 
{{< /katex >}}m_2 = \tan\theta_2
{{< /katex >}}

First we assume that, {{< katex display=true >}}
l_1{{< katex display=true >}}
 and 
{{< /katex >}}l_2
{{< /katex >}} are perpendicular to each other.

Then {{< katex display=true >}}
\angle ABC = 90° - \theta_1{{< katex display=true >}}
 (sum of angles of {{< katex display=true >}}
\triangle ABC
{{< /katex >}} is 
{{< /katex >}}180°
{{< /katex >}})

Now measuring slope of {{< katex display=true >}}
l_2{{< katex display=true >}}
 through angles {{< katex display=true >}}
\theta_2
{{< /katex >}} and 
{{< /katex >}}90° - \theta_1
{{< /katex >}}, which are opposite to each other, we get:

{{< katex display=true >}}
\tan\theta_2 = -\tan(90° - \theta_1) = -\frac{\sin(90° - \theta_1)}{\cos(90° - \theta_1)} = -\frac{\cos\theta_1}{\sin\theta_1} = -\cot\theta_1
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\tan\theta_2 = -\frac{1}{\tan\theta_1}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\tan\theta_1 \cdot \tan\theta_2 = -1
{{< /katex >}}

Thus, when the line {{< katex display=true >}}
l_1{{< katex display=true >}}
 is perpendicular to line {{< katex display=true >}}
l_2
{{< /katex >}} then 
{{< /katex >}}m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1
{{< /katex >}}.

### Conversely

Let {{< katex display=true >}}
l_1{{< katex display=true >}}
 and {{< katex display=true >}}
l_2{{< katex display=true >}}
 be two non-vertical lines with slopes {{< katex display=true >}}
m_1
{{< /katex >}} and 
{{< /katex >}}m_2
{{< /katex >}} respectively, such that 
{{< /katex >}}m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1
{{< /katex >}}.

Since {{< katex display=true >}}
m_1 = \tan\theta_1{{< katex display=true >}}
, 
{{< /katex >}}m_2 = \tan\theta_2
{{< /katex >}}

We have {{< katex display=true >}}
\tan\theta_1 \cdot \tan\theta_2 = -1
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\tan\theta_2 = -\frac{1}{\tan\theta_1} = -\cot\theta_1 = \tan(90° + \theta_1)
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\theta_2 = 90° + \theta_1
{{< /katex >}}

But in {{< katex display=true >}}
\triangle ABC{{< katex display=true >}}
, 
{{< /katex >}}\theta_2 = \angle C + \theta_1
{{< /katex >}}

Therefore, {{< katex display=true >}}
\angle C = 90°
{{< /katex >}}

> **Note:** In any triangle, exterior angle is equal to sum of the interior opposite angles.

> **KNOW?** Let {{< katex display=true >}}
l_1{{< katex display=true >}}
 and {{< katex display=true >}}
l_2{{< katex display=true >}}
 be two lines with well-defined slopes 
{{< /katex >}}m_1
{{< /katex >}} and 
{{< /katex >}}m_2
{{< /katex >}} respectively, then:
> - (i) {{< katex display=true >}}
l_1{{< katex display=true >}}
 is parallel to {{< katex display=true >}}
l_2
{{< /katex >}} if and only if 
{{< /katex >}}m_1 = m_2
{{< /katex >}}.
> - (ii) {{< katex display=true >}}
l_1{{< katex display=true >}}
 is perpendicular to {{< katex display=true >}}
l_2
{{< /katex >}} if and only if 
{{< /katex >}}m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1
{{< /katex >}}.

### Example 5.16

Prove analytically that the line segment joining the mid-points of two sides of a triangle is parallel to the third side and is equal to half of its length.

**Solution:** Let {{< katex display=true >}}
P(a,b){{< katex display=true >}}
, {{< katex display=true >}}
Q(c,d)
{{< /katex >}} and 
{{< /katex >}}R(e,f)
{{< /katex >}} be the vertices of a triangle.

Let {{< katex display=true >}}
S{{< katex display=true >}}
 be the mid-point of {{< katex display=true >}}
PQ{{< katex display=true >}}
 and 
{{< /katex >}}T
{{< /katex >}} be the mid-point of 
{{< /katex >}}PR
{{< /katex >}}.

Therefore,

{{< katex display=true >}}
S = \left(\frac{a+c}{2}, \frac{b+d}{2}\right) \text{ and } T = \left(\frac{a+e}{2}, \frac{b+f}{2}\right)
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\text{Slope of } ST = \frac{\frac{b+f}{2} - \frac{b+d}{2}}{\frac{a+e}{2} - \frac{a+c}{2}} = \frac{f-d}{e-c}
{{< /katex >}}

And slope of {{< katex display=true >}}
QR = \frac{f-d}{e-c}
{{< /katex >}}

Therefore, {{< katex display=true >}}
ST{{< katex display=true >}}
 is parallel to 
{{< /katex >}}QR
{{< /katex >}}. (since, their slopes are equal)

Also,

{{< katex display=true >}}
ST = \sqrt{\left(\frac{a+e}{2} - \frac{a+c}{2}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{b+f}{2} - \frac{b+d}{2}\right)^2}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
= \sqrt{\frac{(e-c)^2}{4} + \frac{(f-d)^2}{4}} = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{(e-c)^2 + (f-d)^2} = \frac{1}{2}QR
{{< /katex >}}

Thus {{< katex display=true >}}
ST{{< katex display=true >}}
 is parallel to 
{{< /katex >}}QR
{{< /katex >}} and half of it.
